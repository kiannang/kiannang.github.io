There are moments in STEM where the work itself is not the hardest part. The deadlines, the experiments, the papers. Those are hard, but they follow a kind of logic. You learn the rules. You get better. But some challenges don't have a rubric, and they don't show up in your research group's Slack channel.

Sometimes the hardest part is figuring out how to advocate for yourself without being labeled difficult. Sometimes it's trying to stay composed when something genuinely stings. Sometimes it's the quiet, exhausting loop of asking: *Am I overreacting? Am I too sensitive? Should I have spoken up earlier?*

This is the part no one fully discusses.

## The Moment That Made Me Write This

Not long ago, I found myself outside a decision that affected a project I cared deeply about. Not a small logistical thing. Something that touched authorship, ownership, and the direction of work I had genuinely invested myself in. I felt frustrated. I felt hurt. I felt angry. And almost immediately, I started policing those feelings.

> Was I making something personal out of something that was supposed to be professional? Or was I finally noticing something I should have named a long time ago?

That internal interrogation, the second-guessing of your own emotional response before you've even processed it, is something I've come to recognize as its own kind of burden. It's not unique to me. I think many women in STEM learn early to monitor not just what they say, but how they might be perceived for saying it. Directness becomes aggression. Emotion becomes instability. Wanting credit becomes ego. Setting a boundary becomes not being a team player.

So instead of asking *"Was this situation fair?"* we ask *"Am I allowed to feel this way?"*

I'm trying to learn that the answer can be yes.

## Learning to Hold Two Things at Once

Yes, I can be emotional and still be rational.

Yes, I can feel hurt and still communicate professionally.

Yes, I can respect someone's intentions and still name their impact on me.

Yes, I can want collaboration without wanting my work to disappear into someone else's timeline.

Most situations aren't cleanly good or bad. A project merge might make strategic sense. A colleague might be under real pressure. A professor might believe they're making the work stronger. All of that can be true, and I can still feel excluded if a decision gains momentum before I'm meaningfully included. Things coexist. They have to.

What I'm relearning is what professionalism actually means. It isn't the absence of emotion. It's what you do with emotion once it shows up. It's taking a walk before you reply. It's waiting until you can write the message clearly. It's asking for clarity instead of making accusations. It's saying, *"This affects my role in the project, and I would like to be part of the conversation."*

That is not unprofessional. That is self-advocacy.

## The Invisible Benchmark

STEM has this quiet culture of constant output. Someone around you is always in full grind mode, and without anyone saying anything, it becomes the standard. You start feeling like needing rest, or just time to *think*, is somehow falling behind.

> Someone else's urgency does not have to become your identity.

I can care deeply and still want reasonable expectations. I can work hard and still need to be included in decisions. I can be ambitious and still want my labor recognized clearly. These are not contradictions. They're just things nobody says out loud enough.

## On Anger, and What It's Actually Telling You

Anger is the emotion I'm quickest to shame myself for. But I'm learning that anger is information. It's a signal that a boundary has been crossed, that something feels unfair, that you're being asked to accept a situation that doesn't sit right. The goal isn't to let anger run the conversation. The goal is to listen to what it's pointing toward. Then respond in a way that protects both your integrity and your relationships.

I'm also trying to trust my instincts more. Not blindly, and not without reflection, but with the recognition that my gut has gotten me this far. It's helped me choose the right projects, ask the questions that mattered, notice when something was worth paying attention to, and keep going in rooms where I didn't always feel completely at ease. My instincts aren't infallible, but they're worth listening to. I'm done apologizing for that.

## The Balancing Act Nobody Names

Being a woman in STEM often means learning to occupy a very narrow space: be kind but not passive, confident but not arrogant, emotional but not dismissed, collaborative but not invisible. There's no script for it. Some days I navigate it gracefully. Some days I have to step away and breathe before I can respond. Some days I need someone to tell me that what I'm feeling makes sense.

I don't think that makes me weak. I think it makes me human.

And maybe growing in STEM isn't about stripping away that humanity. It's about learning to carry it with more trust. Trust that asking for clarity is allowed. Trust that advocating for yourself isn't selfishness. Trust that professionalism can include honesty, boundaries, and yes, emotion.

I don't have to choose between being thoughtful and being strong.

I don't have to choose between being sensitive and being competent.

There is space for all of it.

There has to be.
